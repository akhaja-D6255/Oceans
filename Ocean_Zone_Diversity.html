Ocean Zones & Their Diversity

Zone Name Depth Range Sunlight Biodiversity & Organisms Exploration Status Energy Production Creature Lifestyle & Adaptations
Sunlight Zone (Epipelagic/Surface Zone) 0 – 200 m Plentiful sunlight; Full sunlight - Highest diversity
- Home to coral reefs, small fish, dolphins, sharks, turtles, sea birds
- Producers like phytoplankton grow by photosynthesis 		Fully Explored Photosynthesis:
Primary producers (phytoplankton, algae, seaweeds) use sunlight, water, and CO₂ to make food and oxygen. 		Active, migratory, and fast-growing.
Warm, oxygen-rich waters with abundant food.
Foundation of the marine food chain.
Twilight Zone (Mesopelagic Zone) 200 – 1,000 m Very little light; Not enough for photosynthesis - Moderate diversity
- Inhabitants include squid, lanternfish, sperm whales (that dive to hunt), and bioluminescent organisms
- Adapted to very low light conditions 		Partially Explored Relies on energy from organic matter (marine snow) falling from the Sunlight Zone. Adapted to low-light conditions.
Use bioluminescence for communication and hunting.
Generally slower than surface creatures.
Midnight Zone (Bathypelagic Zone) 1,000 – 4,000 m No sunlight - Low biodiversity with specialized species
- Examples: anglerfish, giant squid, viperfish, and deep-sea jellyfish
- Life relies on marine snow and predation on other deep-sea creatures 		Very Limited Exploration Energy comes from marine snow: organic debris and dead matter sinking from above. Slow metabolism and specialized structures.
Use bioluminescence to attract prey and communicate.
Adapted to cold, dark, and high-pressure environments.
Abyss Zone (Abyssopelagic Zone) 4,000 – 6,000 m No sunlight - Very low biodiversity
- Unique species like basket starfish, sea cucumbers, and deep-sea worms
- Relies mainly on marine snow falling from above 		Mostly Unexplored Energy mainly comes from marine snow; minimal local chemosynthetic production. Adapted to extreme pressure and near-freezing temperatures.
Slow metabolism to conserve energy.
Lives in a very harsh environment.
Hadal Zone (Trenches/Hadalpelagic Zone) 6,000 – 11,000+ m Total darkness - Extremely low biodiversity
- Inhabitants include hadal snailfish, amphipods, and microorganisms
- Many organisms remain unknown due to limited exploration 		Almost Unexplored Relies on both marine snow and chemosynthesis by bacteria near hydrothermal vents. Extreme adaptations for high pressure and near-freezing temperatures.
Slow metabolism with minimal movement.
Unique and strange forms of life.
